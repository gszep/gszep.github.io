In June 2025, a Divisional Court of Sharp P and Johnson J handed down judgment in [*R (Ayinde) v London Borough of Haringey* [2025] EWHC 1383 (Admin)](https://www.bailii.org/ew/cases/EWHC/Admin/2025/1383.html). The grounds for judicial review cited five authorities. None of them existed. One was attributed to the Court of Appeal. When opposing counsel pointed this out, the barrister's response was a letter the court later described as "quite remarkable" — complaining about the focus on "errors in citations" which could, apparently, be "easily explained", though no explanation was offered. The companion judgment in *Al-Haroun v Qatar National Bank* — heard the same day — went further: of forty-five authorities cited, eighteen were non-existent, and many of the real cases contained none of the quotations attributed to them. Wasted costs orders of £2,000 plus VAT were made against counsel personally and equivalent sums against the solicitors. The barrister and solicitors were referred to their regulators. The court held the contempt threshold to have been met, but declined to pursue contempt proceedings, citing inexperience and the lawyers' admitted negligence — and made it explicit that this was not a precedent.

Two months later, in [*MS (Bangladesh) v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2025] UKUT 305 (IAC)](https://www.bailii.org/uk/cases/UKUT/IAC/2025/305.html), an immigration barrister handed up nine stapled pages to the Upper Tribunal, presented as a judgment of the Court of Appeal. They were a ChatGPT print-out. The case at the heart of the argument did not exist; the panel found that a fictitious *Y (China)* had been bolted on to the citation of an unrelated decision. The detail that ought to chasten anyone working in this space: when the barrister was given a copy of *Ayinde* and asked to reconsider the citation, he used ChatGPT to verify it, and ChatGPT obligingly confirmed the fake. Another referral to the Bar Standards Board.

These are not isolated incidents. The [public tracker](https://www.damiencharlotin.com/hallucinations/) of judgments containing AI-fabricated authorities counted around sixty in the United Kingdom by the end of March 2026, and new cases arrive most weeks. The Bar Council updated its [generative AI guidance](https://www.barcouncilethics.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/Considerations-when-using-ChatGPT-and-generative-artificial-intelligence-Nov-2025.pdf) in late November 2025, in part to keep pace with the case law. The professional question — what counts as adequate verification, who carries the duty, what sanction follows breach — has stopped being theoretical.

I want to talk about why this keeps happening, what the architecture of a tool that does not do this looks like, and a deeper observation about a professional heuristic that has quietly broken.

## Why fluent critique is so often wrong

The pattern in *Ayinde* and *MS* — fluent prose, well-formatted citations, internally coherent argument, none of it tied to anything real — is a predictable failure mode of large language models, not an aberration. The clinical word "hallucination" is misleading. What the model is doing is closer to confabulation: producing content that fits the local statistics of plausible legal writing without any anchor in retrieved fact.

Some of this is straightforwardly an artefact of training. Cases, judgments and statutes are sparse in pre-training data relative to the volume of *talk about* cases, judgments and statutes. The model has seen far more legal commentary than it has seen the underlying authorities; what it has learnt to generate is the texture of commentary. Asked to cite, it produces things that look like citations. Asked to summarise a decision it has never seen, it produces a summary in the shape of summaries it has seen. In the absence of grounding, fluency is the default mode of failure.

Retrieval helps, but does not solve the problem. The Stanford RegLab study by Magesh and colleagues, [*Hallucination-Free? Assessing the Reliability of Leading AI Legal Research Tools*](https://law.stanford.edu/publications/hallucination-free-assessing-the-reliability-of-leading-ai-legal-research-tools/), tested the major retrieval-augmented commercial systems and found Lexis+ AI hallucinating on roughly 17% of queries, Westlaw's AI-Assisted Research on about 33%, and a vanilla GPT-4 baseline on around 43%. Adding retrieval cuts the rate, but at one in three for the leading tools, it does not produce something a barrister can rely on without independent verification.

There is a sharper failure mode that matters more for our purposes, and it is the one at issue when a draft is handed to a model for *critique* rather than for drafting. Asked to attack a document, a current frontier model is exceptionally good at producing critique-shaped text: identifying weak phrasings, suggesting counter-arguments, flagging missing authorities. It is also exceptionally good at producing such critique with no grounding whatsoever in the underlying facts of the matter. A point that "looks like" a strong objection — internally coherent, fluently expressed, structurally well-formed — can be wrong on every factual particular at once. The model has no mechanism for telling the difference, because the same statistical machinery generates both.

The recent literature on multi-agent systems has been mapping these failure modes. Wynn, Satija and Hadfield's [*Talk Isn't Always Cheap*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.05396) showed that having models discuss an answer can reduce accuracy rather than improve it: agents drift from correct positions toward incorrect ones in response to peer reasoning, with the dynamics dominated by sycophancy and conformity rather than by argument quality. Princeton's [*AI Agents That Matter*](https://agents.cs.princeton.edu/), from Kapoor, Stroebl, Siegel, Nadgir and Narayanan, made the related point at the level of evaluation: when cost is held constant, many elaborate multi-agent architectures fail to outperform simple baselines that call a single strong model multiple times. More agents does not mean less hallucination; it can mean more confident hallucination at higher cost.

The conclusion the literature converges on is not that LLMs cannot be made reliable, but that reliability has to be engineered into the *structure* of how they are used. The single-shot critique pattern — paste in the document, ask for criticisms, read the output — has no such structure. It is the worst possible configuration for legal work: high-stakes, fact-dense, retrieval-poor, with the model rewarded for sounding right and punished for nothing.

## What actually works

The architecture that does work, in the systems I have built and seen built, is some version of a synthesiser-and-auditor pattern. The names vary; the structural commitments are the same.

The first commitment is **information asymmetry between roles**. The agent that drafts or critiques does not also verify. The agent that verifies does not see the drafting agent's output unfiltered; it sees a decomposition of that output into checkable propositions. This is the design at the heart of the [MARCH framework](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24579) — Multi-Agent Reinforced Self-Check — where a Solver generates, a Proposer decomposes the output into atomic claims, and a Checker validates each claim against retrieved evidence in isolation, deprived of the Solver's original wording. The information asymmetry is the point: it is what breaks the self-confirmation loop in which a verifier rationalises the generator's mistakes because it has been shown the generator's reasoning.

The second is **atomic claim decomposition**. Critique that says "the section on causation is weak" is not checkable. Critique that says "the proposition on page twelve, paragraph four — that *Caparo* requires foreseeability of the *type* of damage rather than the *extent* — misstates the authority" is checkable: the source says what it says. The architectural commitment is that no critique leaves the system in a form that is not reducible to a list of atomic, individually verifiable propositions, each annotated with the source it would stand or fall on. The [PROClaim work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28488) — a courtroom-style framework with progressive retrieval — took this approach to its logical conclusion, achieving 81.7% accuracy on the [Check-COVID](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18265) benchmark, around ten points above standard multi-agent debate, with most of the gain attributable to the decomposition-plus-retrieval step rather than to the debate itself.

The third is **heterogeneous tools and models**. If the same model both generates and verifies, you have not introduced a check; you have introduced a second sample from a correlated distribution. The [Tool-MAD framework](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04742) makes this explicit: distinct agents are bound to distinct external tools — one to a vector-indexed corpus of authorities, another to a live legal search API — and their disagreements are surfaced rather than averaged away. The principle generalises. Where it is feasible, use different model families for the synthesiser and the auditor, and bind each to different evidence sources.

The fourth is **bounded debate**. The empirical finding in *Talk Isn't Always Cheap* is that more rounds of inter-agent discussion do not help and frequently hurt. The right design choice is to cap interaction at one or two structured exchanges, with the loop terminated by a deterministic procedure rather than by the agents declaring agreement. Agreement is not evidence of truth in this regime; agreement is what sycophancy produces.

The fifth is **human prioritisation as the gate**. The final move in the pipeline is not the auditor's verdict; it is a ranked list of contested propositions, each with the source it depends on, presented to a human reviewer for decision. The system never autonomously concludes that a piece of critique is correct. It produces a *worklist*: here are the points the auditor cannot verify, here is the source it would need to verify them, here is the rank order in which a human should look. Everything downstream is a barrister or solicitor reading authorities and exercising judgement, with the tool functioning as a search-and-shortlist accelerator rather than a deliverer of opinions.

These five commitments are sufficient to produce a system whose output is *inspectable*. A reviewer can see, for any claim the system has made, which atomic propositions it rests on, which sources support them, where the auditor disagreed, where it abstained. There is no opaque "the AI thinks this is wrong" step. The trade-off is that the system is slower, more expensive per query, and less impressive in demonstration. It is also the only configuration I am willing to put in front of a barrister with a duty to the court.

The literature in this space is moving fast and not all of it is honest about its trade-offs. The headline figures from multi-agent debate papers tend to be on benchmarks such as Check-COVID or AVeriTeC where the ground truth is known; legal work is not such a benchmark, and a system that achieves 80% on Check-COVID is not 80% reliable on a Court of Appeal skeleton. The right way to read those papers is for *which architectural choices* improved accuracy, not for absolute numbers. Atomic decomposition helps. Heterogeneous evidence helps. Information asymmetry helps. Open-ended debate does not. That is the engineering content.

## The other side of the bench

Most of the public discussion of legal AI focuses on what counsel does. The harder problem is what counsel's clients do.

Sophisticated commercial clients — and increasingly unsophisticated ones — now have direct access to frontier models. They will use them. They are using them. A general counsel reading a draft skeleton will paste it into a model and ask for criticisms; a finance director will run the model over a settlement letter; a board member will get a model to "review" the advice that has just been delivered. The output looks expert. It is often wrong in ways the client cannot detect, because the failure mode of these models on legal documents is exactly the failure mode the client has no training to spot — fluent confabulation that mimics the shape of expertise.

The realistic professional response is not to wish this away. Clients are not going to stop. The response is to build, or recommend, tools that channel client-side AI use toward grounded outputs whose findings are inspectable. A client-facing tool that produces the same kind of decomposed, source-anchored worklist as the auditor side — "here are the four points my model raised, here is the authority each rests on, here is what the actual authority says" — gives the practitioner something to work with. The conversation moves from "your draft is wrong, here is six pages from a chatbot saying so" to "your model raised four points; three are based on misreadings of the underlying authority, the fourth is worth discussing". The first conversation cannot be had productively. The second can.

There is a wider movement that this connects to, sometimes called Rules-as-Code or computational law. The [Catala project](https://catala-lang.org/) at Inria — used in pilots [with CNAF and DGFiP](https://www.inria.fr/en/catala-software-dgfip-cnaf) to express French family-benefits and income-tax computations as executable code derived directly from the underlying legislation — and the [DC Council's GitHub-versioned](https://github.com/DCCouncil/dc-law) Code of the District of Columbia are early examples of a different model of legal infrastructure: authorities expressed in machine-readable form, queryable and version-controlled rather than retrieved as prose. Where these approaches are available, the auditor's job becomes much easier: a claim about what a statute says can be checked against the statute as code, not against a model's recollection of its prose. Most of legal practice will not be amenable to this for a long time, but where it is amenable, the architectural advantages compound.

## The heuristic that no longer holds

This next part matters most, and has the least to do with engineering.

For most of the history of professional services, well-structured critique was strong evidence that the critic had done the work. A six-page memo identifying a dozen flaws in a draft, written in clean prose, with citations and case references, could be wrong on the merits, but could not be casually generated. Producing critique of that quality required reading the underlying documents, understanding the legal framework, doing the research. The form was a costly signal of the substance.

That signal is broken. A six-page memo of fluent critique — well-organised, citation-dense, internally coherent — can now be produced in under a minute by someone who has read none of the underlying material. The structure of the critique is no longer evidence about the critic. The form has decoupled from the substance because a generator now exists that produces the form essentially for free.

The professional adaptation this requires is harder than learning a new tool. It is *unlearning* a heuristic that has been a reliable shortcut for the entire careers of every senior practitioner alive. Senior counsel are accustomed to triaging junior work by reading the structure: a well-organised note suggests a well-organised mind, and the substance can be skimmed because the structure has done the screening. This used to be efficient. It is now a vulnerability. The same structure is now produced, indistinguishably, by something that has done no thinking at all.

What replaces the old heuristic is not a return to reading everything in full. That is not a real option for any practice with volume. What replaces it is an explicit verification step that does the work the structure used to do for you: pick a sample of the claims, trace them to source, check whether they hold. Either you do this manually as a habit, or you delegate it to a tool whose architecture forces it on the model — which is what the synthesiser-and-auditor pattern is, in essence: an automated implementation of the verification step that the broken heuristic used to make optional.

The same logic applies in reverse. A junior who produces a memo with the help of an LLM and submits it without doing the verification step is no longer doing legal work; they are doing prose generation. The output may pass the senior's old heuristic. It will not pass any heuristic that has been updated for the world we now inhabit. *Ayinde* and *MS* are what happens when neither side updates.

## What this does to the junior pipeline

There is a question that sits underneath all of this and that I do not think has a settled answer. Junior lawyers are partly *trained* by doing the work that AI is now plausibly able to do — first-cut research, summarising authorities, drafting routine sections, finding the cases. That work has historically been the apprenticeship. Take it away, and the senior practitioner of 2040 has not had the formative experience that the senior practitioner of 2020 had.

Two responses are coherent. The first is that the technology should be deployed to *augment* juniors — to give them better tools for doing harder work earlier, raise the floor of what counts as junior output, and use the time saved on routine work for deeper engagement with the matters that matter. On this view the apprenticeship survives in altered form: less rote research, more substantive reasoning, with the AI shouldering the parts that were never really developmental.

The second is that the technology should be deployed to *replace* juniors — fewer pupillages and trainee contracts, more leveraged seniors, work routed through tools rather than through people. On this view the apprenticeship contracts. The pipeline narrows.

Both are commercially viable in the short run. They produce different professions in the long run. Which one obtains is a choice the profession will make — mostly through individual hiring decisions and individual training-budget decisions, in aggregate, over the next decade. The choice is not a technical question. The same tooling supports either. Engineers can build for both, and will build for whichever the profession buys.

I think the augmentation path is the better one, and not only because I find it more attractive. The replacement path produces a profession whose seniors lack the formative experience that produced their current seniors, with effects that are very hard to predict and likely to surface late. The augmentation path is more conservative in the strict sense: it preserves the existing structure while raising the floor of what each layer can do. But the choice will not be made by people who think about it carefully. It will be made by people responding to the next quarter's costs.

## In summary

Single-shot LLM critique fails by producing fluent confabulation. Retrieval reduces but does not eliminate this. A synthesiser-and-auditor architecture, with information asymmetry, atomic claim decomposition, heterogeneous tools, bounded debate and a human prioritisation gate, produces output that is at least inspectable. The deeper point is that the professional heuristic which read structure as evidence of substance has broken, and the response is not better prompts but a different architecture for doing the work. The interesting engineering is in the verification step.
